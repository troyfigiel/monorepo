#+title: Binomial distribution

The binomial distribution \( \mathrm{B}(n, p) \) describes the probability distribution for the number of successes for \( n \) independent experiments all having a probability \( p \) of succeeding.

If we let \( n \) grow such that \( n p \) approaches the limit \( \lambda \), the [[id:7757b0ea-1d9b-4cda-bc27-4f911a6df1d4][law of rare events]] states we recover the Poisson distribution \( \mathrm{Pois}(\lambda) \).

When \( n \) increases, the [[id:0c2cc166-7d28-494f-9d7b-bc3fcf393a7f][normal approximation of the binomial]] states that \( \mathcal{N}(np, np(1-p)) \) becomes a reasonable approximation to \( \mathrm{B}(n, p) \).

Proving that the probability mass function of the binomial distribution sums to one, requires a
special case of the [[id:97f3ca80-62e8-4387-82c6-39bb019c513f][Binomial formula]].
